*PHEWWW PAK*

The most cheat-like abuses of physics, like portal loops, have all been patched out of this world through specific game mechanic implementations. However, fundamental physics still apply and remain somewhat abuseable with magic. Like, for instance, the Metal Bullet spell that Mary just used.

The scientific breakdown for why her spell works so well given how little mana is required to cast it is simply a matter of science. Newton's second law of motion implies that smaller objects require less force to move at higher speed than larger objects. Thus, by concentrating more telekinetic force into a smaller object, greater speeds can be achieved, a result that some might consider an abuse case.

Of course, Mary doesn’t feel that this is abusive at all as there are much more sinister ways to make others suffer. She shifts her application of mana from specific locations on the ring of water to a specific location itself. This action change is a subtle difference in technique. However, it completely changes the application of energy onto the water to a substantially more efficient method.

To really understand this difference between the methods, imagine a pinwheel. If you spin it with your hands, then it will move fast. But, there is an upper limit for how fast you can make it based on how fast you can flick the wheel. Instead, to avoid that upper limit, you learn to blow on it instead, allowing you to spin the pinwheel much faster than otherwise.

The difference between the two scenarios is the efficiency of the application of force. Blowing on the pinwheel is not producing some astronomically greater force than using ones hands. Actually, one would probably expect hands to be producing more overall force than one’s lungs. It’s simply the case that blowing applies force to the pinwheel more effectively.

This is basically what Mary is doing. Since Mary is controlling force telekinetically using magic, what she does is effectively the same as switching from holding onto the wheel and moving it to pushing it outright. And, doing this has drastic results.

To a casual observer, her water ring isn’t moving at all. It is a thin stream of water that doesn’t interfere with light, so one’s eyes won’t notice anything peculiar about it.

She uses a thin vacuum around the water to further reduce friction with the surrounding atmosphere as she continuously applies force to bring the water to an extreme rate of speed.

Anybody who has seen a simple steam engine in action can appreciate what is going on here. Still, the game developers have been quite thorough with their balancing of the world and there is another limit to how fast she can spin the water. Currently, she is utilizing magic to telekinetically suspend the water in the air. And, as the water spins, it brushes against that, causing the stability of her very telekinesis spell to waver. Thus, even with Mary changing her application of force and reducing friction with air, the stability of the spell begins to waver once more.

*PAP PAP PAP PAP PAP*

In this world, there are two measurement systems for, grade and tier. Grade is qualitative measure of strength, while tier is a quantitative measure. And, the two can be thought of ‘the quality of’ and ‘the amount of’.

For tiers, power is ranked on a scale from Tier 1 (T1) to Tier Infinity (T-), with Tier 10 being the generally highest acclaimed tier and every tier being separated by a multiplier of 5. This puts T1 at 1, T2 at 5, T3 at 25 and so on and so forth.

In contrast, grade uses vague measurements specific to what it is measuring and have 10 values too ranging from introductory to Divine. For items, a grade may refer to the overall value, sophistication and power level of an enchantment. Or, for spells, it may refer to its complexity, difficulty and mana requirements to cast.

In Mary’s case, her magic casting ability is skilled. However, to be considered a Skilled Mage, one needs a T4 mana capacity too. Thus, she is skilled in one way, but not in another. Perspective, conventions and opinions matter a lot for grades.
